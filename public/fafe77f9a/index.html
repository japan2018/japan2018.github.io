#Clifford Algebra

It is a calculation method that allows geometrical objects (length, area, volume, etc.) to be handled in an algebraic way.

A brief explanation will be given to the extent required this time.

basis vector

The vector that represents the axes is called the basis vector. Three-dimensional $xyz$ coordinate, $x$ axis basis vector is $\mathbf{e_1}$, $y$ axis basis vector is $\mathbf{e_2}$, $z$ axis basis vector Be $\mathbf{e_3}$.

\mathbf{e_1},\mathbf{e_2},\mathbf{e_3}=
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right)

1-vector

Basis vectors allow you to write vectors in algebraic style.

\begin{align}
   \left(\begin{matrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{matrix}\right)
&= \left(\begin{matrix} x \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 + \left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ y \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 + \left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ z \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=x\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +y\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +z\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=x\mathbf{e_1}+y\mathbf{e_2}+z\mathbf{e_3}
\end{align}

Here is a concrete example.

\left(\begin{matrix} 2 \\ 3 \\ 4 \end{matrix}\right)
=2\mathbf{e_1}+3\mathbf{e_2}+4\mathbf{e_3}

A vector represented by a linear combination of basis vectors (constant multiple and sum) is called a 1-vector in Clifford algebra terminology.

##2-vector

In everyday life, think of units as follows.

  • Length (m) x Length (m) = Area (㎡)

Applying this to the basis, we can think of:

  • Length ($\mathbf{e_1}$) x Length ($\mathbf{e_2}$) = Area ($\mathbf{e_1e_2}$)

$\mathbf{e_1e_2}$ is the basis for the area, and the coefficient is the area value.

Here is a simple example.

(2\mathbf{e_1})(3\mathbf{e_2})=6\mathbf{e_1e_2}

The left side represents a rectangle with sides 2 and 3, and the right side $6$ represents the area.

The area expressed by combining two bases in this way is called a 2-vector in Clifford algebra terminology. Just as a 1-vector represents the size (length) of a line segment, a 2-vector represents the size (area) of a surface.

The cross product of two 1-vectors that are skewed to each other represents the area of the parallelogram they span.

orientation

Just as a 1-vector is a length with an orientation, a 2-vector is an area with an orientation. The width of the expression changes depending on the dimension.

2D

Since the parallelogram always lies in the $xy$ plane, the orientation is represented by a sign. The sign is affected by how the base is taken. It is positive if the two 1-vectors that form the surface have the same positional relationship as the basis (counterclockwise in the right-handed system).

*Specific shapes will be posted later as the geometric product of two components.

3D

The surface on which the parallelogram rests faces in various directions. The orientation is expressed by the ratio of the component to the coordinate plane.

*Specific shapes will be posted later as a geometric product of three components.

##3-vector

With the same idea, a 3-vector representing a volume can be defined. This time, we assume 3D because 4D and above are not covered.

Here is a simple example.

(2\mathbf{e_1})(3\mathbf{e_2})(4\mathbf{e_3})=24\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}

The left side represents a rectangular parallelepiped with sides 2, 3 and 4, and $24$ on the right side represents the volume.

The cross product of three 1-vectors that are diagonal to each other represents the volume of the parallelepiped they span.

  • Corresponds to scalar triple product in vector analysis and determinant of cubic square matrix. Details are omitted because it exceeds the range of this time.

The orientation is represented by a sign in a 3-vector. The sign is affected by how the base is taken. It is positive if the three 1-vectors that form the surface have the same positional relationship as the basis. It’s a little difficult to imagine, but you should be aware of the behavior that the sign is inverted by mirror image inversion.

  • It behaves in the same way as a 2-dimensional 2-vector, but both are pseudo-scalars, which will be described later. In general, an n-dimensional n-vector is a pseudoscalar. One-dimensional 1-vectors can be seen that the direction is represented by a sign by imagining an arrow on the number line.

Expression expansion

By writing in the style of algebraic expressions, you can treat vector multiplication as if it were an ordinary algebraic expression.

For comparison, consider the expansion of a normal expression without a vector.

2 variables

>>> from sympy import *
>>> x,y=symbols("x y")
>>> a,b,c,d=symbols("a b c d")
>>> (a*x+b*y)*(c*x+d*y)
(a*x + b*y)*(c*x + d*y)
>>> expand((a*x+b*y)*(c*x+d*y))
a*c*x**2 + a*d*x*y + b*c*x*y + b*d*y**2
>>> expand((a*x+b*y)*(c*x+d*y)).collect(x*y)
a*c*x**2 + b*d*y**2 + x*y*(a*d + b*c)

\begin{align}
&(ax+by)(cx+dy) \\
&=ax(cx+dy) \\
&\quad +by(cx+dy) \\
&=acx^2+adxy \\
&\quad +bc\underbrace{yx}_{xy}+bdy^2 \\
&=acx^2+bdy^2+\underbrace{(ad+bc)xy}_{Crosterm}
\end{align}

Note that we use commutativity $xy=yx$ to group similar terms.

3 variables

>>> x,y,z=symbols("x y z")
>>> a1,a2,a3,b1,b2,b3=symbols("a1:4 b1:4")
>>> (a1*x+a2*y+a3*z)*(b1*x+b2*y+b3*z)
(a1*x + a2*y + a3*z)*(b1*x + b2*y + b3*z)
>>> expand((a1*x+a2*y+a3*z)*(b1*x+b2*y+b3*z))
a1*b1*x**2 + a1*b2*x*y + a1*b3*x*z + a2*b1*x*y + a2*b2*y**2 + a2*b3*y*z + a3*b1*x*z + a3*b2*y*z + a3*b3*z**2
>>> expand((a1*x+a2*y+a3*z)*(b1*x+b2*y+b3*z)).collect([x*y,y*z,z*x])
a1*b1*x**2 + a2*b2*y**2 + a3*b3*z**2 + x*y*(a1*b2 + a2*b1) + x*z*(a1*b3 + a3*b1) + y*z*(a2*b3 + a3*b2)

\begin{align}
&(a_1+a_2y+a_3z)(b_1x+b_2y+b_3z) \\
&=a_1x(b_1x+b_2y+b_3z) \\
&\quad +a_2y(b_1x+b_2y+b_3z) \\
&\quad +a_3z(b_1x+b_2y+b_3z) \\
&=a_1b_1x^2+a_1b_2xy+a_1b_3\underbrace{xz}_{zx} \\
&\quad +a_2b_1\underbrace{yx}_{xy}+a_2b_2y^2+a_3b_3yz \\
&\quad +a_3b_1zx+a_3b_2\underbrace{zy}_{yz}+a_3b_3z^2 \\
&=a_1b_1x^2+a_2b_2y^2+a_3b_3z^2 \\
&\quad +\underbrace{(a_1b_2+a_2b_1)xy+(a_2b_3+a_3b_2)yz+(a_3b_1+a_1b_3)zx}_{Crosterm} \\
\end{align}

The variables of the cross term are arranged cyclically ($x→y→z→x→\cdots$).

Geometric product

Clifford algebra provides geometric products for vector multiplication. This allows you to calculate inner and outer products simultaneously from two vectors.

Products of different bases combine.

The product of the same basis is $1$. This is the rule for calculating the dot product.

[Example] \ \mathbf{e_1e_1}=1

If you change the order of the combined bases, the sign will be inverted. This is called anti-commutability. This is the rule for calculating the cross product.

[Example] \ \mathbf{e_1e_2}=-\mathbf{e_2e_1}
  • The outer product here is the same as the type called wedge product.

2 ingredients

Two-dimensional 1-vectors are represented by two components. Calculate their geometric product. Compare it with the expansion of the two-variable expression.

\begin{align}
&(a\mathbf{e_1}+b\mathbf{e_2})(c\mathbf{e_1}+d\mathbf{e_2}) \\
&=a\mathbf{e_1}(c\mathbf{e_1}+d\mathbf{e_2}) \\
&\quad +b\mathbf{e_2}(c\mathbf{e_1}+d\mathbf{e_2}) \\
&=ac\underbrace{\mathbf{e_1e_1}}_{1}+ad\mathbf{e_1e_2} \\
&\quad +bc\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_1}}_{-\mathbf{e_1e_2}}+bd\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_2}}_{1} \\
&=\underbrace{(ac+bd)}_{inner product}+\underbrace{(ad-bc)\mathbf{e_1e_2}}_{outer product} \\
\end{align}

It can be confirmed that the inner product can be obtained from the rule that the same basis is $1$ and the outer product can be obtained from the anticommutative property. The cross product is the cross term.

The cross product coefficient $ad-bc$ is the area of a parallelogram spanned by two 1-vectors. This matches the determinant of a matrix of two 1-vectors side by side.

\det\left(\begin{array}{c|c}a&c\\b&d\end{array}\right)=ad-bc

3 ingredients

A 3-dimensional 1-vector is represented by 3 components. Calculate their geometric product. Compare it with the expansion of the three-variable expression above.

\begin{align}
&(a_1\mathbf{e_1}+a_2\mathbf{e_2}+a_3\mathbf{e_3})(b_1\mathbf{e_1}+b_2\mathbf{e_2}+b_3\mathbf{e_3}) \\
&=a_1\mathbf{e_1}(b_1\mathbf{e_1}+b_2\mathbf{e_2}+b_3\mathbf{e_3}) \\
&\quad +a_2\mathbf{e_2}(b_1\mathbf{e_1}+b_2\mathbf{e_2}+b_3\mathbf{e_3}) \\
&\quad +a_3\mathbf{e_3}(b_1\mathbf{e_1}+b_2\mathbf{e_2}+b_3\mathbf{e_3}) \\
&=a_1b_1\underbrace{\mathbf{e_1e_1}}_{1}+a_1b_2\mathbf{e_1e_2}+a_1b_3\underbrace{\mathbf{e_1e_3}}_{-\mathbf{e_3e_1}} \\
&\quad +a_2b_1\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_1}}_{-\mathbf{e_1e_2}}+a_2b_2\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_2}}_{1}+a_2b_3\mathbf{e_2e_3} \\
&\quad +a_3b_1\mathbf{e_3e_1}+a_3b_2\underbrace{\mathbf{e_3e_2}}_{-\mathbf{e_2e_3}}+a_3b_3\underbrace{\mathbf{e_3e_3}}_{1} \\
&=\underbrace{(a_1b_1+a_2b_2+a_3b_3)}_{ dot product} \\
&\quad +\underbrace{(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf{e_1e_2}+(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\mathbf{e_2e_3}+(a_3b_1-a_1b_3)\mathbf{e_3e_1}}_{exterior} \\
\end{align}

The outer product has three components because it represents a parallelogram spanned by two vectors by projecting it onto the plane on the $xy,yz,zx$ coordinate axes represented by the basis of the 2-vector. The orientation of the surface is expressed by three components.

The area of the parallelogram is obtained as the square root of the sum of the squares of the three components.

\sqrt{(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)^2+(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)^2+(a_3b_1-a_1b_3)^2}
  • The calculation method is the same as the straight line length $\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$. In the vector product represented by the pseudo vector described later, the length represents the area, but it is based on the same idea.

Formal sum

All three-dimensional bases are eight, including unlabeled scalars. Formally arrange everything in polynomial form. (This polynomial is called formal sum)

\underbrace{a_0}_{scalar}+\underbrace{a_1\mathbf{e_1}+a_2\mathbf{e_2}+a_3\mathbf{e_3}_{1-vector}+\underbrace{a_4\mathbf{e_1e_2} +a_5\mathbf{e_2e_3}+a_6\mathbf{e_3e_1}}_{2-vector} +\underbrace{a_7\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}}_{3-vector}

Pseudo-vector/pseudo-scalar

Focusing on the number of terms, the scalar and the 3-vector have one term, and the 1-vector and the 2-vector have three terms, which are the same number.

Because of the same number of terms, we sometimes treat a 2-vector as a pseudo 1-vector. A 1-vector derived from such a 2-vector is called a pseudovector. Typical pseudovectors appear as a type of outer product called a vector product.

*The correspondence between 1-vectors and 2-vectors will be explained later as Hodge dual.

Similarly, you might treat a 3-vector as pseudo-scalar. A scalar derived from such a 3-vector is called a pseudoscalar.

\underbrace{a_0}_{scalar}+\underbrace{a_1\mathbf{e_1}+a_2\mathbf{e_2}+a_3\mathbf{e_3}}_{vector}+\underbrace{a_4\mathbf{e_1e_2}+a_5 \mathbf{e_2e_3}+a_6\mathbf{e_3e_1}}_{pseudovector}+\underbrace{a_7\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}}_{pseudoscalar}

A three-dimensional pseudovector corresponds to the normal to the surface represented by the 2-vector.

  • The expression of the surface by the normal line depends on the property that the number of 1-vector and 2-vector components is the same in 3D, so it does not hold in general dimensions. For details, please refer to Cross product and pleasant friends.

#Pauli Matrix

In the above explanation, 1-vector is expressed by vector, but 2-vector and 3-vector are shown as algebraic expressions. Isn’t it possible to express these as a matrix of the same size and calculate the geometric product as it is by multiplying the matrices? In other words, can Clifford algebra be represented by a matrix?

Since the formal sum is 8 terms, we need $ℝ^8$ amount of information to store all the coefficients. A square matrix is easy to handle to calculate the product of matrices, so a quadratic complex square matrix will fit.

Find such a matrix. And see that it is called the Pauli Matrix**.

Scalar

The base of the scalar is $1$. Assign the identity matrix because it is the identity unit of the multiplication.

1↦I=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix}\right)

1-vector

Complex each base, add more columns and fill with unknowns.

\begin{align}
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right)
&\xrightarrow{Complex}
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} i \\ 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{matrix}\right) \\
&\xrightarrow{More columns}
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & x_1 \\ 0 & y_1 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} i & x_2 \\ 0 & y_2 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & x_3 \\ 1 & y_3 \end{matrix}\right)
\end{align}

Find the solution because the square of the basis is $1$. Since solve specifies the equation where $=0$, the identity matrix _1 on the right side is transposed to the left side to form -_1.

>>> x1,x2,x3,y1,y2,y3=symbols("x1:4 y1:4")
>>> _1=eye(2)
>>> solve(Matrix([[1,x1],[0,y1]])**2-_1,[x1,y1])
[(0, -1), (0, 1)]
>>> solve(Matrix([[I,x2],[0,y2]])**2-_1,[x2,y2])
[]
>>> solve(Matrix([[0,x3],[1,y3]])**2-_1,[x3,y3])
[(Ten)]

\begin{align}
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & x_1 \\ 0 & y_1 \end{matrix}\right)^2&=I&
∴(x_1,y_1)&=(0,-1),(0,1) \\
\left(\begin{matrix} i & x_2 \\ 0 & y_2 \end{matrix}\right)^2&=I&
∴(x_2,y_2)&= no solution \\
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & x_3 \\ 1 & y_3 \end{matrix}\right)^2&=I&
∴(x_3,y_3)&=(1,0)
\end{align}

$(x_1,y_1)$ has two solutions, but $(0,1)$ is the identity matrix and is already assigned to the scalar, so we choose $(0,-1)$.$(x_2,y_2)$ has no solution. If you check the matrix elements, you can see that they are not satisfied before the unknowns are calculated because the constant elements are different.

>>> Matrix([[I,x2],[0,y2]])**2
Matrix((
[-1, x2*y2 + I*x2],
[0, y2**2]])

\left(\begin{matrix} i & x_2 \\ 0 & y_2 \end{matrix}\right)^2
=\left(\begin{matrix} -1 & x_2y_2+x_2i \\ 0 & y_2^2 \end{matrix}\right)
≠ \left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix}\right)

This doesn’t align the bases, so move $i$ to the second line instead.

>>> x4,y4=symbols("x4 y4")
>>> solve(Matrix([[0,x4],[I,y4]])**2-_1,[x4,y4])
[(-I, 0)]

\begin{align}
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & x_4 \\ i & y_4 \end{matrix}\right)^2&=I&
∴(x_4,y_4)&=(-i,0)
\end{align}

Now you have 3 bases. Let’s say $E$.

>>> E=[Matrix([[1,0],[0,-1]]),Matrix([[0,1],[1,0]]),Matrix([[0,-I] ,[I,0]])]

E_0,E_1,E_2:=
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)

We’re off our initial assumptions, so we need to consider which one we want to assign to $\mathbf{e_1},\mathbf{e_2},\mathbf{e_3}$.

##3-vector

To determine the representation matrix of the 1-vector basis, consider the 3-vector basis.

In the Clifford algebra, squaring the 3-vector basis $\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}$ yields $-1$. You can confirm it by repeating the exchange in the same way as the bubble sort.

\begin{align}
(\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3})^2
&=\mathbf{e_1e_2}\underbrace{\mathbf{e_3e_1}}_{exchange}\mathbf{e_2e_3} \\
&=-\mathbf{e_1}\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_1}}_{exchange}\mathbf{e_3e_2e_3} \\
&=\mathbf{e_1e_1e_2}\underbrace{\mathbf{e_3e_2}}_{exchange}\mathbf{e_3} \\
&=-\underbrace{\mathbf{e_1e_1}}_{1}\underbrace{\mathbf{e_2e_2}}_{1}\underbrace{\mathbf{e_3e_3}}_{1} \\
&=-1
\end{align}

Since $\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}$ is $-1$ when squared, it is equated with the imaginary number $i$ ($\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}=i$).

  • For details, please refer to Hodge dual of Euclidean space and bubble sort.

E_0E_2E_1
&=\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 =\left(\begin{matrix}-i & 0 \\ 0 &-i \end{matrix}\right)=-iI \\
E_1E_0E_2

  \left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 =\left(\begin{matrix}-i & 0 \\ 0 &-i \end{matrix}\right)=-iI \\
E_1E_2E_0
&=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)
 =\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{matrix}\right)=iI \\
E_2E_0E_1
&=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 =\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{matrix}\right)=iI \\
E_2E_1E_0
&=\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
  \left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)

\end{align}

It turns out that three combinations of $E_0E_1E_2,E_1E_2E_0,E_2E_0E_1$ are $i$ times the identity matrix.

*The combination of $iI$ is right-handed. The arrangement is cyclic ($0→1→2→0→1→\cdots$). The same type of expression can be obtained by selecting any combination.

Selection

The rest is a matter of decision. From the appearance, $E_1$ has a simple shape (unsigned, mirror-inverted identity matrix), so we choose this as the expression matrix for $\mathbf{e_1}$. If $\mathbf{e_1}$ is decided, the latter combination will be decided automatically. This combination is called Pauli matrix and is written as $σ_1,σ_2,σ_3$.

>>> s1,s2,s3=Matrix([[0,1],[1,0]]),Matrix([[0,-I],[I,0]]),Matrix([[1, 0],[0,-1]])

\mathbf{e_1},\mathbf{e_2},\mathbf{e_3} \mapsto σ_1,σ_2,σ_3 :=
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right)

3-Check the representation of the basis of the vector again.

\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}=i \quad\cong\quad σ_1σ_2σ_3=iI

In this article, $\mathbf{e_n}$ and $σ_n$ are used as follows.

  • $\mathbf{e_n}$: Clifford algebra calculation
  • $σ_n$: Expression matrix calculation

Hodge dual

If we use the property that the geometric product of the same basis is $1$, we can break the 3-vector basis $i=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}$ into a 2-vector basis.

\begin{align}
i\mathbf{e_1}&=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3e_1}=\mathbf{e_1e_1e_2e_3}=\mathbf{e_2e_3} \\
i\mathbf{e_2}&=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3e_2}=-\mathbf{e_1e_2e_2e_3}=-\mathbf{e_1e_3}=\mathbf{e_3e_1} \\
i\mathbf{e_3}&=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3e_3}=\mathbf{e_1e_2}
\end{align}

The bases multiplied by $i$ cancel each other out, so the remaining bases remain. This complementary relationship is called Hodge dual.

\mathbf{e_1} \overset{Hodge dual}{\longleftrightarrow} \mathbf{e_2e_3} \\
\mathbf{e_2} \overset{Hodge dual} {\longleftrightarrow} \mathbf{e_3e_1} \\
\mathbf{e_3} \overset{Hodge dual}{\longleftrightarrow} \mathbf{e_1e_2}
  • The operation to obtain the 1-vector as the Hodge dual of the 2-vector is not simply to multiply the imaginary number. For details, please refer to Hodge dual of Euclidean space and bubble sort.

Let’s do the same calculation with the Pauli matrix.

>>> I*s1
Matrix((
[0, I],
[I, 0]])
>>> I*s2
Matrix((
[0, 1],
[-Ten]])
>>> I*s3
Matrix((
[I, 0],
[0, -I]])
>>> I*s1==s2*s3
True
>>> I*s2==s3*s1
True
>>> I*s3==s1*s2
True

iσ_1,iσ_2,iσ_3=
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\-1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right),
\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 &-i \end{matrix}\right) \\
iσ_1=σ_2σ_3,\ iσ_2=σ_3σ_1,\ iσ_3=σ_1σ_2

The Clifford algebra erases by exchanging the bases, but the Pauli matrix only multiplies the components by the imaginary number. The interesting thing is that the calculation results are completely different, but the same results are obtained. Looking at only the calculation of the Pauli matrix does not show that the 3-vector basis is broken, so it can be interpreted by combining it with the viewpoint of the Clifford algebra.

Summary

In Clifford algebra, the standard notation is that the bases are arranged (eg $\mathbf{e_1e_2}$). On the other hand, in the calculation of Pauli matrix, it is standard to calculate components and extract $i$ as a common factor.

Indicates the base correspondence.

Quaternion

The square of a 2-vector is $-1$. It may be easier to understand using the Pauli matrix.

(\mathbf{e_1e_2})^2=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_1e_2}=-\mathbf{\underbrace{e_1e_1}_{1}\underbrace{e_2e_2}_{1}}=-1 \\
(iσ_3)^2=\underbrace{i^2}_{-1}\underbrace{σ_3^2}_{1}=-1

Since there are 3 types of 2-vector basis, it is likely to correspond to the quaternion $i,j,k$.

sign

Try to reproduce $ij=k$. However, if you simply apply it, you will get a minus.

(\mathbf{e_2e_3})(\mathbf{e_3e_1})=\mathbf{e_2e_3e_3e_1}=-\mathbf{e_1e_2} \\
(iσ_1)(iσ_2)=i^2σ_1σ_2=-iσ_3

Then, with the idea of inversion, we will add a minus from the beginning to the basis corresponding to $i,j,k$.

\begin{align}
i,j,k
&\mapsto -\mathbf{e_2e_3},-\mathbf{e_3e_1},-\mathbf{e_1e_2} \\
&\mapsto -iσ_1,-iσ_2,-iσ_3
\end{align}

Summary

Quaternions correspond to scalars (signs unchanged) and 2-vectors (sign inversions).

\begin{align}
&\underbrace{a}_{scalar}+\underbrace{bi+cj+dk}_{2-vector} \\
&\mapsto a-b\mathbf{e_2e_3}-c\mathbf{e_3e_1}-d\mathbf{e_1e_2} \\
&\maps to a I-biσ_1-ciσ_2-diσ_3 \\
&=a\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix}\right)
 -b\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 -c\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\-1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 -d\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 &-i \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=a \underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 1& 0 \\ 0& 1 \end{matrix}\right)}_{1}
 +b \underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-i \\-i& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{i}
 +c \underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-1 \\ 1& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{j}
 +d \underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix}-i& 0 \\ 0& i \end{matrix}\right)}_{k} \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix}a-di & -bi-c \\ -bi+c & a+di\end{matrix}\right) \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix}(a+di)^* & -(c+bi) \\ (c+bi)^* & a+di\end{matrix}\right)
\end{align}

Let’s use Clifford algebra as a reference.

\begin{align}
&a+b\mathbf{e_1e_2}+c\mathbf{e_2e_3}+d\mathbf{e_3e_1} \\
&\maps to aI+biσ_3+ciσ_1+diσ_2 \\
&\maps to a-bk-ci-dj
\end{align}

Dual quaternion

Interpreting the quaternion with the Clifford algebra revealed that it corresponds to the scalar and the 2-vector. However, since it is incomplete as a Clifford algebra, we will consider how to extend the quaternion to handle 1-vectors.

You can convert it to a 1-vector by multiplying the 2-vector by an imaginary number (3-vector basis).

i(\mathbf{e_2e_3})=\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3e_2e_3}=-\mathbf{e_1e_2e_2e_3e_3}=-\mathbf{e_1} \\
i(iσ_1)=-σ_1

*The sign is reversed, but this is not a Hodge dual. For details, please refer to Hodge dual of Euclidean space and bubble sort.

Adding a new 3-vector basis, the imaginary number $h$, to the quaternion makes it isomorphic to the Clifford algebra by combining with the existing element $i,j,k$. Such extended quaternions are called biquaternions.

\begin{align}
&(a_0+a_1i+a_2j+a_3k)+\underbrace{(a_4+a_5i+a_6j+a_7k)}_{Composite}\underbrace{h}_{Addition} \\
&=\underbrace{a_0}_{scalar}+\underbrace{a_1i+a_2j+a_3k}_{2-vector}+\underbrace{a_4h}_{3-vector}+\underbrace{a_5hi+a_6hj+a_7hk}_ {1-vector}
\end{align}

Check the correspondence between $hi,hj,hk$ and Pauli matrices.

\begin{align}
hi &\mapsto \underbrace{i}_{h}\underbrace{(-iσ_1)}_{i}=σ_1 \\
hj &\mapsto \underbrace{i}_{h}\underbrace{(-iσ_2)}_{j}=σ_2 \\
hk &\mapsto \underbrace{i}_{h}\underbrace{(-iσ_3)}_{k}=σ_3 \\
∴hi,hj,hk &\maps to σ_1,σ_2,σ_3
\end{align}

The correspondence with Clifford algebra and Pauli matrix based on the dual quaternion is as follows.

\begin{align}
&\underbrace{a_0}_{scalar}+\underbrace{a_1i+a_2j+a_3k}_{2-vector}+\underbrace{a_4h}_{3-vector}+\underbrace{a_5hi+a_6hj+a_7hk}_{ 1-vector} \\
&\mapsto a_0-a_1\mathbf{e_2e_3}-a_2\mathbf{e_3e_1}-a_3\mathbf{e_1e_2}+a_4\mathbf{e_1e_2e_3}+a_5\mathbf{e_1}+a_6\mathbf{e_2}+a_7\math {e_3} \\
&\maps to a_0I-a_1iσ_1-a_2iσ_2-a_3iσ_3+a_4iI+a_5σ_1+a_6σ_2+a_7σ_3 \\
&=a_0\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix}\right)
 -a_1\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 -a_2\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\-1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 -a_3\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 &-i \end{matrix}\right) \\
&\quad
 +a_4\left(\begin{matrix} i & 0 \\ 0 & i \end{matrix}\right)
 +a_5\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +a_6\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +a_7\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=a_0\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 1& 0 \\ 0& 1 \end{matrix}\right)}_{1}
 +a_1\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-i \\-i& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{i}
 +a_2\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-1 \\ 1& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{j}
 +a_3\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix}-i& 0 \\ 0& i \end{matrix}\right)}_{k} \\
&\quad
 +a_4\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} i& 0 \\ 0& i \end{matrix}\right)}_{h}
 +a_5\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0& 1 \\ 1& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{hi}
 +a_6\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-i \\ i& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{hj}
 +a_7\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 1& 0 \\ 0&-1 \end{matrix}\right)}_{hk} \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix} a_0-a_3i+a_4i+a_7 & -a_1i-a_2+a_5-a_6i \\ -a_1i+a_2+a_5+a_6i & a_0+a_3i+a_4i-a_7 \end{matrix}\ right) \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix} (a_0+a_7)-(a_3-a_4)i & -\{(a_2-a_5)+(a_1+a_6)i\} \\ (a_2+a_5)-(a_1 -a_6)i & (a_0-a_7)+(a_3+a_4)i \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix} \{(a_0+a_7)+(a_3-a_4)i\}^* & -\{(a_2-a_5)+(a_1+a_6)i\} \\ \{ (a_2+a_5)+(a_1-a_6)i\}^* & (a_0-a_7)+(a_3+a_4)i \end{matrix}\right)
\end{align}

Let’s use Clifford algebra as a reference.

\begin{align}
&a_0+a_1\mathbf{e_1}+a_2\mathbf{e_2}+a_3\mathbf{e_3}+a_4\mathbf{e_1e_2}+a_5\mathbf{e_2e_3}+a_6\mathbf{e_3e_1}+a_7\mathbf{e_1e_2e_2e \\
&\maps to a_0I+a_1σ_1+a_2σ_2+a_3σ_3+a_4iσ_3+a_5iσ_1+a_6iσ_2+a_7iI \\
&\maps to a_0+a_1hi+a_2hj+a_3hk-a_4k-a_5i-a_6j+a_7h
\end{align}

Thus, dual quaternions, three-dimensional Clifford algebras, and Pauli matrices are isomorphic. From this point of view, it can be said that the target that the quaternion handles is a three-dimensional Euclidean space, and the quaternion is completed in the form of a biquaternion.A quadratic form of $hi,hj,hk$ in a biquaternion is a linear form of $\mathbf{e_1},\mathbf{e_2},\mathbf{e_3}$ in a Clifford algebra, and vice versa. It is interesting to note that the first-order form $i,j,k$ of numbers is the second-order form $\mathbf{e_3e_2},\mathbf{e_1e_3},\mathbf{e_2e_1}$ of Clifford algebra (Quaternion and Clifford Two-vectors of algebra have different signs, but they are expressed by permuting the order due to anticommutative properties). It seems to be confusing what is atomic and what is complex as a concept, but I feel that it is better to follow the Clifford algebraic interpretation if it has a geometric meaning.

Decomposition quaternion

In the previous article (Bi-complex numbers considered by expression matrix), I saw that bi-complex numbers and two-dimensional Clifford algebras almost correspond, There was a difference in some calculation results. On the other hand, if you limit the dual quaternion to two dimensions, you will get a perfect isomorphism.

2-dimensional 1-vector can be handled only by $hj,hk$. The only 2-vector resulting from the compound is $i$. There are no 3-vectors in 2D because 2-vectors are pseudoscalars.

\underbrace{a}_{scalar}+\underbrace{bi}_{2-vector}+\underbrace{chj+dhk}_{1-vector}
  • 1-The original choice used for the vector is arbitrary. There is no problem even if you use another combination ($hi,hj$, etc.), but as you see below, the correspondence will be difficult to understand by changing the letters.

For convenience, the letters are replaced. The number obtained by limiting the dual quaternion to two dimensions and changing the letters is called a decomposition quaternion.

a+bi+chj+dhk\ \mapsto\ a+bi+cj+dk
  • Since $j,k$ is assigned to 1-vector for historical reasons, we chose $hj,hk$ whose correspondence is easy to understand.

The correspondence with the Clifford algebra and Pauli matrices based on the decomposition type quaternion is as follows. Unlike bi-complex numbers, it is completely isomorphic.

  • In order to compare with $hj,hk$ and Pauli matrix in 3 dimensions, the following formula intentionally uses $\mathbf{e_2},\mathbf{e_3}$. Originally, it should be expressed as $\mathbf{e_1},\mathbf{e_2}$ in 2 dimensions.
\begin{align}
&\underbrace{a}_{scalar}+\underbrace{bi}_{2-vector}+\underbrace{cj+dk}_{1-vector} \\
&\mapsto a-b\mathbf{e_2e_3}+c\mathbf{e_2}+d\mathbf{e_3} \\
&\maps to aI-biσ_1+cσ_2+dσ_3 \\
&=a\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{matrix}\right)
 -b\left(\begin{matrix} 0 & i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +c\left(\begin{matrix} 0 &-i \\ i & 0 \end{matrix}\right)
 +d\left(\begin{matrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 &-1 \end{matrix}\right) \\
&=a\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 1& 0 \\ 0& 1 \end{matrix}\right)}_{1}
 +b\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-i \\-i& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{i}
 +c\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 0&-i \\ i& 0 \end{matrix}\right)}_{j}
 +d\underbrace{\left(\begin{matrix} 1& 0 \\ 0&-1 \end{matrix}\right)}_{k} \\
&=\left(\begin{matrix} a+d & -bi-ci \\ -bi+ci & a-d \end{matrix}\right)
\end{align}
  • I said that the choice of basis is arbitrary, but if you use $hk,hi$ (depending on the cyclic ordering), all the elements of the expression matrix will be real numbers.

Let’s use Clifford algebra as a reference.

\begin{align}
&a+b\mathbf{e_2}+c\mathbf{e_3}+d\mathbf{e_2e_3} \\
&\maps to aI+bσ_2+cσ_3+diσ_1 \\
&\maps to a+bi+cj-dk
\end{align}
  • Decomposition-type quaternion was introduced in the sense of recovery of a hint by a bicomplex number. I think it is easier to use Clifford algebra in practice.
