#Understanding exponential distribution

What is exponential distribution

\begin{equation}
f(x)=
    \left\{
    \begin{aligned}
          &\lambda \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x} &(x\geq0) \\
          &0 &(x<0)\\
    \end{aligned}
    \right.
\end{equation}

The exponential distribution is a probability distribution that indicates the probability that the occurrence interval of events that average $\lambda$ times per unit time is $x$ unit time**. The probability density function is given as above.

The exponential distribution is used in the following examples.

  • Interval between disasters
  • The interval between accidental failures of a system with constant failure time
  • Interval from one customer to the next at the store

It also has the property that the expected value is $\frac{1}{\lambda}$ and the variance is $\frac{1}{\lambda^2}$.

Shape of exponential distribution

Now let’s actually draw the distribution. Let’s draw the “probability distribution of the time interval until the next customer comes to the store” by considering the following three examples.

  • Stores with an average of 5 people per hour ($\lambda = 5$)
  • Stores with an average of 10 people per hour ($\lambda = 10$)
  • Stores with an average of 15 people per hour ($\lambda = 15$)
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def exp_dist(lambda_, x):
    
    return lambda_ * np.exp(- lambda_*x)

x = np.arange(0, 1, 0.01)
y1= [exp_dist(5,i) for i in x]
y2= [exp_dist(10,i) for i in x]
y3= [exp_dist(15,i) for i in x]

plt.plot(x, y1, color="red"
                ,alpha=0.5, label="exp_dist λ= %d" %5)

plt.plot(x, y2, color="green"
                ,alpha=0.5, label="exp_dist λ= %d" %10)

plt.plot(x, y3, color="blue"
                ,alpha=0.5, label="exp_dist λ= %d" %15)

plt.legend()
plt.show()

Cumulative distribution function of exponential distribution

What I would like to know more as our daily feeling is the probability that the next customer will come within 10 minutes, rather than the probability that the next customer will come exactly 10 minutes later. Therefore, consider the probability that the event occurrence interval is within $x$ unit time. It is necessary to add the probabilities together, that is, to obtain them by integration.

{\begin{eqnarray}

F(x) &=& \int_0^x f(x) dx \\
     &=& \int_0^x \lambda \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x} dx\\
     &=& \lambda\int_0^x \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x} dx \\
     &=& \lambda\left[\frac{1}{-\lambda}\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}\right]^x_0 \\
     &=& -\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}-(-1) \\
     &=& 1-\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x} \\


\end{eqnarray}}

This cumulative distribution function is also drawn by considering the following three examples.

  • Stores with an average of 5 people per hour ($\lambda = 5$)
  • Stores with an average of 10 people per hour ($\lambda = 10$)
  • Stores with an average of 15 people per hour ($\lambda = 15$)
def cum_exp_dist(lambda_, x):
    
    return 1-np.exp(-lambda_ * x)

x = np.arange(0, 1, 0.01)
y1= [cum_exp_dist(5,i) for i in x]
y2= [cum_exp_dist(10,i) for i in x]
y3= [cum_exp_dist(15,i) for i in x]

plt.plot(x, y1, color="red"
                ,alpha=0.5, label="cum_exp_dist λ= %d" %5)

plt.legend()

Derivation of exponential distribution

Now let’s consider derivation of the exponential distribution.

It is assumed that the event does not occur until the time $x$, and the event occurs for the first time in the section of $x$ and $x+Δx$. ($Δx$ is a minute section) Then, the probability that an event will occur for the first time in the section of $x$ and $x+Δx$ can be expressed by the following equation.


f(x)Δx = (1-F(x))・\lambdaΔx

From the definition of the probability density function, it can be understood that $f(x)Δx$ represents the probability that an event will occur during $Δx$. The right side is the probability that an event will not occur by $x$ ($(1-F(x))$) and the probability that an event will occur between $x$ and $x+Δx$ ($\lambdaΔx$) I am multiplying.

Because of the memorylessness, the probability of an event occurring up to $x$ and the probability of an event occurring between $x$ and $x+Δx$ are independent and can be multiplied as they are.

Then I will write below where $\lambdaΔx$ comes from. The minute section $Δx$ is divided into $n$ equal units ($n$ is large enough) for one unit time, so the following holds.


Δx・n=1

This time, since we consider events that occur on average $\lambda$ times in unit time, considering the probability that events will occur in each minute interval using the above equation, it will be as follows.


p = \frac{\lambda}{n} = \lambdaΔx

Now you know the meaning of $\lambdaΔx$. Now let’s expand the formula given above.

{\begin{eqnarray}


f(x)Δx &=& (1-F(x))・\lambdaΔx \\
   f(x)&=& \lambda-\lambda F(x) \\
   f'(x)&=& -\lambda f(x)

\end{eqnarray}}

In the expansion from the second line to the third line, both sides are differentiated by $x$.

Although $f’(x)= -\lambda f(x)$ is in the form of differential equation, it is easy to assume that it is an exponential function considering that it is a function that does not change even if it is differentiated. I understand.


f(x) = C・\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}

```$C$ is a constant and can be anything, but it needs to be a cumulative distribution function when integrated. Therefore, there is a constraint that $\int_0^∞f(x)=1$ is inevitable. The value of the constant $C$ is determined using the constraint.

```math
{\begin{eqnarray}

\int_0^∞f(x) &=& \int_0^∞ C・\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x} \\
     &=& C\int_0^∞ \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}\\
     &=& C\left[\frac{1}{-\lambda}\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}\right]^∞_0 \\
&&C\left[\frac{1}{-\lambda}\mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}\right]^∞_0 \\
&&=&-C(\frac{1}{-\lambda})\\
&&=&frac{C}{\lambda}\\
        \\
1 &=& \frac{C}{\lambda}

\end{eqnarray}}

Since there is a constraint that $\int_0^∞f(x)=1$,

{\begin{eqnarray}

1 &=& \frac{C}{\lambda}　\\
C &=& \lambda\\

\end{eqnarray}}

From the above, we were able to derive $f(x) = \lambda \mathrm{e}^{-\lambda x}$.

Maximum likelihood estimation of exponential distribution parameters

Next, let us consider the maximum likelihood estimation of the parameters of the exponential distribution. **Maximum likelihood estimation is the likelihood function $L(\theta;x)=f(x;\theta), where $f(x;\theta)$ is the probability density function that follows a certain parameter $\theta$ The estimator that maximizes $$\theta=\hat\theta$ is called the maximum likelihood estimator. **

Suppose $x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n$ is output when a random number that follows the exponential distribution of $\lambda = \theta$ is generated. Consider the maximum likelihood estimator of $\lambda = \theta$ at this time.


L(\theta ;x) = \theta \mathrm{e}^{-\theta x}

This time, the data is given as $x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n$ and $n$, and they are all generated independently, so the likelihood function can be expressed as follows.


\begin{eqnarray*}L(\theta ;x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n)=
L(\theta ;x_1) × L(\theta ;x_2) × \cdots × L(\theta ;x_n)
\end{eqnarray*}

Applying the above to the exponential distribution results in the following.


\begin{eqnarray*}L(\mu,\sigma ;x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n)=
\displaystyle\prod_{k=1}\theta\mathrm{e}^{-\theta x}

\end{eqnarray*}

It suffices to find $\theta$ that maximizes the above likelihood function, but the same result can be obtained by converting it to a task that finds the maximum value by converting it to a log likelihood function. Convert to degree function.


\begin{eqnarray*}
\log(L(\mu,\sigma ;x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n))&=&
\log(n\theta) + \log( \mathrm{e}^{-\theta (x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n)})\\
&=& n\log(\theta) -\theta (x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n) \\

\end{eqnarray*}

Put $ l(\theta)=\log (L(\theta ;x_1,x_2,\cdots,x_n))$ and differentiate $l(\theta)$ by $\theta$.


{\begin{eqnarray*}
\frac{\partial l(\theta)}{\partial\theta}&=&\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}(n\log(\theta) -\theta (x_1 + x_2 + \cdots , + x_n)) \\
&=& n・\frac{1}{\theta}-(x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n)

\end{eqnarray*}}

From here, put $\frac{\partial l(\theta)}{\partial\theta}=0$ and solve for $\theta$.


{\begin{eqnarray*}

n・\frac{1}{\theta}-(x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n) &=& 0 \\
n・\frac{1}{\theta} &=& (x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n)\\
\theta &=& \frac{n}{x_1 + x_2 + \cdots, + x_n} \\

\end{eqnarray*}}

It turns out that the maximum likelihood estimator for the parameters of the exponential distribution is $\frac{n}{x_1 + x_2 +\cdots,+x_n}$.

Let’s apply this maximum likelihood estimator to a concrete example. Suppose you measure the time between customers at a store five times.

  • Time from opening to arrival of the first person: 20 minutes
  • Time from the first visit to the second visit: 15 minutes
  • Time from the second visit to the third visit: 20 minutes
  • Time from 3rd person to 4th person: 15 minutes
  • Time from the arrival of the 4th person to the arrival of the 5th person: 20 minutes

Given that the store visit interval follows an exponential distribution, the maximum likelihood estimator of the parameter $\lambda$ obtained from the above data is as follows.


{\begin{eqnarray*}

\frac{5}{\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{3} }
\fallingdotseq 3.33

\\

\end{eqnarray*}}

The maximum likelihood estimation based on the given data revealed that the customer visit interval is considered to follow the exponential distribution when the average visit time is $3.33$ per unit time (1 hour).
